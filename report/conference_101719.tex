\begin{abstract}
Neural implicit representations such as DeepSDF learn continuous signed distance functions for 3D shape reconstruction, but require dense signed distance supervision for every training point. We investigate whether Eikonal regularization, which enforces the unit gradient norm constraint on all training points (supervised and unsupervised), can compensate for reduced supervision---and, more broadly, how positional encoding interacts with this setting. We train DeepSDF on 299 ShapeNet shapes (airplane, chair, table) at supervision ratios from 5\% to 100\% and evaluate with Chamfer Distance and Normal Consistency on 299 training-set shapes. An early instance of our preprocessing pipeline sampled supervised SDF labels only in a thin $\pm 0.1$ band around each surface; we found that this induced a degenerate solution where the decoder output $\approx 0$ everywhere and all reconstructions collapsed to near-spherical blobs. After regenerating supervision with an 80/20 mix of near-surface multi-scale offsets and far-field signed-distance samples, retraining produces genuinely shape-specific reconstructions and the experimental signal becomes interpretable. Our main findings from the retrained pipeline are: (i) Eikonal regularization enables substantial label reduction---at 10\% supervision, the 3-seed CD mean (0.0315, CV = 0.047) is lower than the 100\% non-Eikonal baseline (0.0361), while 50\% and 5\% Eikonal runs lie in the same 0.029--0.034 band; (ii) Fourier PE at $L=6$ and 10\% supervision (3-seed mean CD = 0.0308, CV = 0.040) matches the no-PE 10\% CD almost exactly, but halves Normal Consistency (NC = 0.517 vs.\ 0.729)---PE costs surface-normal quality without a corresponding CD benefit; (iii) Reducing PE frequency from $L=6$ to $L=4$ at 10\% supervision degrades CD to 0.0432, so lower-frequency Fourier encoding is strictly worse rather than a safer default. These results are consistent with Eikonal acting as a volumetric geometric prior that replaces missing surface labels, while Fourier PE injects high-frequency artifacts that distort surface normals even when CD stays low.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Learning 3D shape representations from data is a central problem in computer vision with applications in robotics, autonomous driving, and virtual reality. Traditional approaches such as voxel grids and point clouds suffer from resolution limitations and lack of surface continuity. Neural implicit representations address these issues by parameterizing shapes as continuous functions---typically signed distance functions (SDFs)---that can be queried at arbitrary resolution~\cite{deepsdf}.

DeepSDF~\cite{deepsdf} learns a neural network $f_\theta(z, x) \to s$ that maps a latent code $z$ and a 3D coordinate $x$ to a signed distance value $s$. Through an auto-decoder framework, per-shape latent codes are jointly optimized with the network weights, enabling the model to represent a distribution of shapes. However, DeepSDF requires dense signed distance supervision: every training point must have a ground-truth SDF value, which is expensive to obtain for real-world 3D data.

This supervision bottleneck motivates semi-supervised approaches that leverage geometric priors to reduce label requirements. The Eikonal equation $\|\nabla_x f(x)\| = 1$ is a fundamental property of signed distance functions~\cite{igr}. Enforcing this constraint on unsupervised points---those without SDF labels---provides a training signal derived purely from the geometry of the problem. If effective, this could substantially reduce the annotation effort for training neural implicit representations.

Separately, Fourier positional encoding (PE) has been shown to help multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) learn high-frequency functions by mapping low-dimensional inputs to higher-dimensional sinusoidal features~\cite{nerf, fourierfeatures}. While this technique is standard in Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), its interaction with SDF learning---particularly in semi-supervised settings---is less understood.

In this work, we systematically investigate two questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Can Eikonal regularization reduce the need for signed distance supervision in DeepSDF, and by how much?
    \item Does Fourier positional encoding help or hurt reconstruction quality in this semi-supervised SDF setting?
\end{enumerate}

We conduct 10 post-fix experiments on 299 ShapeNet shapes (airplane, chair, table) across supervision ratios from 5\% to 100\%, with one no-Eikonal baseline (EXP-01), Eikonal at multiple ratios, and Fourier PE at two frequency levels. During the study we discovered that the preprocessing pipeline was generating supervised SDF labels only in a thin $\pm 0.1$ band around each surface, which caused the decoder to converge to the trivial near-zero solution and collapse every reconstruction to a sphere (Section~\ref{sec:preprocess_bug}). After fixing preprocessing to include 20\% uniform-in-sphere samples with exact signed distance, retraining produces genuinely shape-specific reconstructions, and the experimental signal becomes interpretable. Our key findings from the retrained pipeline are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Eikonal enables 90\% label reduction}: EXP-04 (10\% supervision, 3 seeds, CD = 0.0315 $\pm$ 0.0015, CV = 0.047) is lower-CD than the 100\% non-Eikonal baseline (EXP-01, CD = 0.0361). EXP-05 (5\% supervision, CD = 0.0344) and EXP-03 (50\%, CD = 0.0288) lie in the same 0.029--0.034 band. Normal Consistency is preserved in the 0.72--0.75 range across all non-PE Eikonal runs. This is the label-efficiency claim the project was designed to test, now validated against a working baseline.
    \item \textbf{Fourier PE is a CD/NC trade, not a free quality gain}: EXP-06 (10\% + PE $L=6$, 3 seeds) has CD = 0.0308 $\pm$ 0.0012, statistically indistinguishable from the no-PE CD at the same ratio, but NC drops to 0.517 $\pm$ 0.006---a $\sim$30\% relative loss in surface-normal accuracy. Lower-frequency PE (EXP-11, $L=4$ at 10\%, CD = 0.0432) is strictly worse on CD, so L=4 is not a safer default. These results reverse the narrative from our pre-fix runs (where sphere-clipped evaluation of degenerate near-zero outputs made PE look artificially competitive).
    \item \textbf{Multi-seed variance is healthy}: both 3-seed configurations (EXP-04 CV = 0.047; EXP-06 CV = 0.040) are well below the project-defined 0.2 threshold, so the central tendency is a real property of the method rather than a seed artifact.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Neural Implicit Representations}

Neural implicit representations encode 3D geometry as the level set of a learned function. Occupancy Networks~\cite{occnet} predict binary inside/outside occupancy, while DeepSDF~\cite{deepsdf} regresses continuous signed distance values, enabling gradient-based surface extraction via marching cubes~\cite{marchingcubes}. DeepSDF's auto-decoder framework avoids the need for an encoder by directly optimizing per-shape latent codes during training. While effective, it requires dense SDF supervision at every training point.

\subsection{Geometric Regularization}

The Eikonal equation $\|\nabla_x f(x)\| = 1$ is a necessary condition for valid signed distance functions. Gropp et al.~\cite{igr} introduced Implicit Geometric Regularization (IGR), demonstrating that enforcing this constraint during training improves surface quality and enables learning from raw point clouds without SDF labels. This approach has been adopted in subsequent work on neural implicit surfaces~\cite{volsdf, neus}. In our work, we investigate whether Eikonal regularization can reduce the required number of supervised training points, assessing its effects across multiple supervision ratios.

SIREN~\cite{siren} takes a different approach to geometric regularization by using periodic activation functions (sinusoidal) instead of ReLU, which naturally satisfies certain differential equation constraints. However, SIREN requires architectural changes, whereas Eikonal regularization can be applied as a loss term to standard ReLU networks.

\subsection{Positional Encoding}

Fourier positional encoding was introduced in NeRF~\cite{nerf} to overcome the spectral bias of MLPs---their tendency to learn low-frequency functions first~\cite{spectralbias}. By mapping input coordinates through sinusoidal functions at multiple frequency bands, $\gamma(x) = [\sin(2^0\pi x), \cos(2^0\pi x), \ldots, \sin(2^{L-1}\pi x), \cos(2^{L-1}\pi x)]$, the network can represent high-frequency variations. Tancik et al.~\cite{fourierfeatures} provided a theoretical framework for this approach based on the Neural Tangent Kernel.

While Fourier features are standard in radiance field methods, their application to SDF learning is less established. NeRF operates in a supervised regime where every ray has a target color, and the network is evaluated primarily at points along training rays. In contrast, SDF evaluation via marching cubes requires querying the network on a dense grid that includes regions far from the training data distribution, creating an extrapolation challenge for high-frequency encodings.

\section{Method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a dataset of 3D shapes, we aim to learn a function $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{d_z} \times \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ that maps a shape-specific latent code $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}$ and a spatial coordinate $x \in \mathbb{R}^3$ to the signed distance to the shape's surface. For each shape $i$, we have:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supervised points}: $\{(x_j, s_j)\}_{j=1}^{N_s}$ with ground-truth SDF values (see Section~\ref{sec:dataset} for the exact sampling procedure)
    \item \textbf{Unsupervised points}: $\{x_k\}_{k=1}^{N_u}$ with coordinates only
\end{itemize}
During preprocessing, each shape is sampled to produce a fixed pool of 250{,}000 supervised points and a separate pool of 250{,}000 unsupervised points. The supervision ratio $r$ controls the fraction of the supervised pool that is retained: at $r = 0.1$ only 25{,}000 supervised points are used, while all 250{,}000 unsupervised points remain available. At $r = 1.0$ the full supervised pool is used alongside the full unsupervised pool. Each training batch samples half its points from the supervised set and half from the unsupervised set. We investigate $r \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.50, 1.0\}$.

\subsection{Architecture}

We use an 8-layer MLP with hidden dimension 512, following the DeepSDF architecture~\cite{deepsdf}. A skip connection concatenates the input to the activations at layer 4. Each shape is associated with a learnable latent code $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$, initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$ and jointly optimized with the network weights (auto-decoder). The final layer produces a scalar SDF value without activation, with weights initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/\sqrt{512})$ and bias set to 0.1 to approximate a sphere.

When Fourier PE is enabled, input coordinates are transformed as:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma(x) = \left[\sin(2^0 \pi x), \cos(2^0 \pi x), \ldots, \sin(2^{L-1} \pi x), \cos(2^{L-1} \pi x)\right]
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the number of frequency levels. This maps each 3D coordinate from $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $\mathbb{R}^{6L}$. The network input becomes $[z_i; \gamma(x)]$ instead of $[z_i; x]$. We test $L=4$ (24-dimensional) and $L=6$ (36-dimensional).

\subsection{Loss Functions}

The total training loss combines four terms:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} + \lambda_{\text{eik}}(t) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}} + \lambda_z \cdot \mathcal{L}_z + \lambda_{\text{2nd}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{SDF loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$): L1 regression on supervised points only:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{j=1}^{N_s} |f_\theta(z_i, x_j) - s_j|
\end{equation}

\textbf{Eikonal loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}}$): Enforces $\|\nabla_x f\| = 1$ on all points (supervised and unsupervised), providing geometric regularization from unlabeled data:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}} = \frac{1}{N_s + N_u} \sum_{k} \left(\|\nabla_x f_\theta(z_i, x_k)\| - 1\right)^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Latent regularization} ($\mathcal{L}_z$): Prevents latent code drift:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_z = \|z_i\|^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Second-order loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}$): Optionally penalizes the divergence of the gradient field to encourage smoother surfaces:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_k |\nabla \cdot \nabla_x f_\theta(z_i, x_k)|
\end{equation}

\subsection{Eikonal Warmup Schedule}

The Eikonal weight uses a linear warmup to prevent the gradient norm constraint from dominating before the network learns basic geometry from $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\text{eik}}(t) = \lambda_{\text{eik}} \cdot \min\left(1, \frac{t}{T_w}\right)
\end{equation}
where $T_w$ is ratio-dependent: 100 epochs for $r \geq 0.5$, 150 epochs for $r = 0.1$, and 200 epochs for $r = 0.05$. Lower supervision ratios require longer warmup because fewer supervised points provide weaker initial geometry signal.

\subsection{Training Procedure}

Training iterates over all shapes per epoch, sampling a batch of 16{,}384 points per shape (split evenly between supervised and unsupervised; exact split not independently documented in the experiment log). We use the Adam optimizer with learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$, stepped by $0.5\times$ every 500 epochs (implemented in training code; this schedule is not independently documented in the project plan or experiment log---the plan specifies only ``Reduce to 1e-4 if training diverges''), and gradient clipping at $\max\_norm = 1.0$.

Default hyperparameters are $\lambda_{\text{eik}} = 0.1$, $\lambda_z = 10^{-4}$, and $\lambda_{\text{2nd}} = 0$ (disabled unless specified). EXP-01 and EXP-02 train for 3{,}000 epochs; the seven priority retrains (EXP-03/04/05/06/11 and multi-seed replicates) train for 1{,}500 epochs to fit the post-fix compute budget.

\section{Experiments}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

We use 300 shapes from ShapeNet~\cite{shapenet} spanning three categories (100 airplanes, 100 chairs, 100 tables). Preprocessing normalizes each mesh to the unit sphere and then samples two point pools per shape:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supervised pool} (250{,}000 points). An 80/20 mix:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \emph{Near-surface (200K)} points generated by multi-scale normal offsets at scales $\epsilon \in \{0.005, 0.01, 0.05\}$ with multipliers $\{-2,-1,1,2\}$, yielding SDF magnitudes up to $|s|=0.1$.
        \item \emph{Far-field (50K)} points sampled uniformly inside the unit sphere with exact signed distance from \texttt{trimesh.proximity.signed\_distance}. Trimesh's convention is positive-inside/negative-outside; we invert the sign to match the project convention (positive-outside/negative-inside).
    \end{itemize}
    Meshes with more than 15{,}000 faces are decimated to 10{,}000 faces before the far-field query to keep preprocessing tractable.
    \item \textbf{Unsupervised pool} (250{,}000 points). Uniform in the unit sphere, coordinates only; used by the Eikonal term.
\end{itemize}
Each mesh is processed with a deterministic per-mesh seed derived from the global seed and the mesh name, so parallel preprocessing is reproducible. One airplane mesh (airplane\_0077) had degenerate triangles that caused \texttt{signed\_distance} to stall beyond 30 minutes; it was dropped from the pipeline, leaving 299 shapes. All 299 shapes are used for training (\texttt{train\_split}~=~1.0); we do not hold out a separate test set in the post-fix pipeline, because the experiment target is a stress test of label efficiency and PE interactions on the training objective rather than generalization to unseen shapes. Reported CD/NC are therefore reconstruction metrics over the training set, and should be interpreted accordingly.

For reduced-supervision runs, supervised points are subsampled to the target ratio (e.g.\ 10\% retains 25{,}000 labeled points) while all 250{,}000 unsupervised points remain available for Eikonal regularization.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

For each trained model we extract a mesh per shape via marching cubes~\cite{marchingcubes} on the SDF field clipped to the unit sphere. All post-fix evaluations are run at resolution $96^3$ to fit the TC2 GPU budget. We report two metrics over the 299 training shapes:

\textbf{Chamfer Distance (CD)}: L1 bidirectional mean nearest-neighbor distance between 30{,}000 points sampled from predicted and ground-truth meshes. Lower is better.

\textbf{Normal Consistency (NC)}: Mean absolute dot product between normals at matched nearest-neighbor pairs. Higher is better (maximum 1.0).

Volumetric IoU and Eikonal Deviation were part of the original plan but were not computed; we discuss this explicitly in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Experiment Design}

Table~\ref{tab:experiments} summarizes the 10 post-fix experiments used in this report. We vary supervision ratio ($r \in \{5\%, 10\%, 50\%, 100\%\}$), Eikonal regularization, and Fourier PE frequency ($L \in \{4, 6\}$) to assess each factor's contribution. Key comparisons are EXP-04 vs.\ EXP-01 (label efficiency with Eikonal vs.\ a fully-supervised non-Eikonal baseline), EXP-06 vs.\ EXP-04 (PE at low supervision), and EXP-11 vs.\ EXP-06 (PE frequency at low supervision). Two configurations were multi-seeded (seeds 42, 123, 456): EXP-04 and EXP-06, both at 10\% supervision, where variance sensitivity is highest. The coefficient-of-variation divergence threshold defined in the project plan is 0.2; both multi-seed configurations are well below this threshold (EXP-04 CD CV = 0.047, EXP-06 CD CV = 0.040) and do not require expansion to 5 seeds.

EXP-07/08/09/10/12 were defined in the original plan but could not be retrained within the post-fix compute window; our conclusions are therefore limited to the 10 runs in Table~\ref{tab:experiments}. In particular, the 100\% + PE rows (EXP-09 for $L=6$, EXP-10 for $L=4$) and the 5\% + PE rows (EXP-07 for $L=6$, EXP-12 for $L=4$) were not run, so the PE-frequency ablation is restricted to the 10\% supervision point.

\subsection{Fourier PE: CD Neutral, NC Negative}
\label{sec:pe_results}

Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation} shows the full ablation across the post-fix runs.
On Chamfer Distance, EXP-06 (10\% + Eikonal + PE $L=6$) matches the no-PE
10\% point almost exactly (0.0308 vs.\ 0.0315 for the 3-seed means), with
overlapping std bars. On Normal Consistency, however, PE $L=6$ drops NC from
0.729 $\pm$ 0.003 (EXP-04) to 0.517 $\pm$ 0.006 (EXP-06)---a clear, consistent,
and reproducible degradation across all three seeds. The 3-seed CV for EXP-06
is 0.040 on CD and 0.013 on NC, so the NC gap is not explained by seed noise.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/ablation_cd_nc.png}
    \caption{Post-fix ablation across the 10 completed runs. Top: Chamfer
    Distance, dashed line marks the EXP-01 baseline. Bottom: Normal
    Consistency. PE $L=6$ at 10\% (6th bar) has the lowest CD of any
    configuration but the lowest NC, highlighting the CD/NC trade-off.}
    \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

The functional interpretation is that Fourier PE encourages the decoder to
fit surface-level scalar SDF values more aggressively in exchange for larger
local gradient variability, which is exactly what degrades normal
consistency: normals are computed from the SDF gradient, and high-frequency
encoding makes that gradient locally noisier even when the level set itself
stays close to the true surface. This is a surface-quality trade, not a
global failure. It reverses our pre-fix conclusion, where PE \emph{appeared}
catastrophic because sphere-clipped evaluation of the near-zero degenerate
field gave nonsensical metrics; with a working baseline, PE's real cost is
visible as NC loss at essentially constant CD.

\subsection{PE Frequency Ablation}

Reducing PE frequency from $L=6$ to $L=4$ at 10\% supervision (EXP-11) raises
CD from 0.0308 to 0.0432 while only recovering NC partway (0.517 $\to$ 0.609,
still below no-PE 0.729). Fig.~\ref{fig:pe_freq} plots the no-PE vs.\ PE
$L=4$ vs.\ PE $L=6$ CD points at 10\%. Lower-frequency PE is therefore not
a gentler, safer alternative; for our preprocessing regime it is strictly
worse on CD without eliminating the NC loss.

\subsection{Multi-Seed Validation}

We validated our two 10\%-supervision workhorse experiments with 3 seeds each
(Table~\ref{tab:multiseed}). Both configurations are well under the
project-defined CV = 0.2 divergence threshold: EXP-04 CD CV = 0.047, EXP-06
CD CV = 0.040. Normal Consistency is even tighter (CV $<$ 0.01 for both).
The central tendencies reported in Section~\ref{sec:eikonal_results} and
Section~\ref{sec:pe_results} are stable across random initialization.

\subsection{The Preprocessing Bug and Its Fix}
\label{sec:preprocess_bug}

The first version of our preprocessing pipeline generated supervised SDF labels only from small normal offsets around each surface. Concretely, for every surface point it produced four offset samples at scales $\epsilon \in \{0.005, 0.01, 0.05\}$ with multipliers $\{-2,-1,1,2\}$, so the entire supervised SDF distribution was concentrated in the thin band $|s| \le 0.1$ with no far-field coverage.

Under L1 SDF loss, latent regularization, and no far-field supervision, this supervised distribution admits a degenerate global optimum: a decoder that outputs $f_\theta(z, x) \approx 0$ for all $x$ and all $z$ has nearly zero loss on every labeled point. In our pre-fix runs, training converged to this solution. The diagnostic symptoms were unambiguous: (i) $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$ plateaued near $0.03$ within a few hundred epochs across every configuration, independent of supervision ratio or PE; (ii) evaluated SDF values on a dense grid were near zero almost everywhere, so marching cubes---clipped to the unit sphere to avoid boundary artifacts---recovered only the clipping sphere itself; (iii) every reconstructed mesh across every category and every configuration was a sphere.

The fix has two parts. First, we extended the supervised sampler with a 20\% far-field slice (50K of the 250K supervised points) drawn uniformly in the unit sphere, with signed distance computed exactly via \texttt{trimesh.proximity.signed\_distance} (sign-inverted to our outside-positive convention). This widens the supervised SDF distribution from $\pm 0.1$ to the full unit-sphere range, so the zero-everywhere solution is no longer close to optimal. Second, we decimate meshes with more than 15{,}000 faces to 10{,}000 faces before the far-field query, because the full ShapeNet topology caused the R-tree-backed \texttt{signed\_distance} call to stall on large meshes; one airplane mesh (airplane\_0077) with degenerate triangles stalled even after decimation and was dropped, leaving 299 shapes.

We validated the fix on a single mesh first: after regenerating its point pool, the supervised SDF distribution spanned roughly $[-0.9, 0.9]$ (vs.\ the old $\pm 0.1$), far-field sign matched the project convention (outside $\to +$, inside $\to -$), and a short sanity training run produced a recognizable airplane geometry rather than a sphere. The full dataset was then regenerated on TC2 and every experiment reported in this paper was retrained from scratch on the new data; pre-fix numbers are not used anywhere in our results tables or figures.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Eikonal Enables Label Reduction}
\label{sec:why_eikonal}

The cleanest claim the post-fix data support is that Eikonal regularization recovers most of the reconstruction quality of a fully-supervised model with only a small fraction of the labels. EXP-04 (10\% + Eikonal, 3-seed CD = 0.0315) beats the non-Eikonal baseline (EXP-01, CD = 0.0361) by about 13\%, and EXP-03 (50\% + Eikonal, CD = 0.0288) beats it by about 20\%. The 5\% + Eikonal run (EXP-05, CD = 0.0344) is also below the 100\% no-Eikonal baseline.

We attribute this to the Eikonal term acting as a volumetric geometric prior. The supervised L1 SDF loss is by construction local: it only constrains $f_\theta$ at $N_s$ labeled points. When $N_s$ is small (25K supervised points per shape at 10\% supervision, 12.5K at 5\%), the decoder has enormous freedom in unsampled regions. The Eikonal term $(\|\nabla_x f\| - 1)^2$ removes that freedom in a specific way: it requires $f_\theta$ to be an honest distance function everywhere the gradient is evaluated, including on the 250K unsupervised points per shape. Practically, this closes two failure modes that hurt low-label DeepSDF: it prevents the decoder from collapsing away from the sparse labels (as the pre-fix preprocessing caused), and it penalizes high-curvature excursions between widely-spaced labeled points. The reason the 10\% + Eikonal run can match or beat 100\% + no-Eikonal is not that 10\% of the labels carry more signal than 100\%; it is that the Eikonal term extracts geometric regularization from the other 250K unsupervised points that the non-Eikonal baseline simply discards.

Two secondary observations support this interpretation. First, Normal Consistency stays in the 0.72--0.75 range across every non-PE Eikonal run regardless of supervision ratio, which is consistent with the Eikonal term enforcing gradient magnitude globally and thereby stabilizing normals even where labels are sparse. Second, the ratio-dependent warmup schedule matters in practice: without warmup, the Eikonal term dominates early training and produces a field with correct gradient magnitude but arbitrary level sets. We did not run a clean warmup ablation in the post-fix window, so this is observational rather than experimentally isolated.

\subsection{Why Fourier PE Costs Normal Consistency}
\label{sec:why_pe}

The post-fix PE result is narrower than our pre-fix conclusion. On Chamfer Distance alone, PE at $L=6$ is statistically indistinguishable from no-PE at the same 10\% supervision ratio (EXP-06 CD = 0.0308 vs.\ EXP-04 CD = 0.0315, both 3-seed, overlapping std). So PE is not ``catastrophic'' for CD in any reasonable sense. What collapses is Normal Consistency: from 0.729 to 0.517 at 10\% supervision, a $\sim$30\% relative loss.

CD is computed on sampled points from the extracted mesh; NC is computed on the mesh's surface normals. A surface can have low CD---its zero level set lies close to the ground-truth surface---while still having noisy normals, because the normal at each surface point is the direction of the SDF gradient at that point, which depends on the local behavior of $f_\theta$, not just on where the level set crosses zero. Fourier PE maps coordinates through sinusoids at frequencies up to $2^{L-1}\pi$; at $L=6$ this injects frequencies up to $32\pi$ into the decoder input. The decoder can use these frequencies to fit the supervised SDF targets more tightly (low CD) while developing high-frequency gradient oscillations tangential to the surface (low NC). This is consistent with what we observe.

The frequency-ablation result sharpens this picture. Reducing $L$ from 6 to 4 at 10\% supervision (EXP-11) does not recover NC to the no-PE level (0.609 vs.\ 0.729) and makes CD worse (0.0432 vs.\ 0.0308). So $L=4$ is not a conservative default; it introduces enough high-frequency modes to damage NC without providing the tighter surface fit that made $L=6$ CD-competitive. Within the frequency range we tested, no PE configuration dominates no-PE on both metrics.

We did not retrain the PE experiments at 100\% or 5\% supervision in the post-fix window, so the CD/NC trade-off characterization is tied to the 10\% point. A complete frequency-sweep at 100\% and 5\%, plus a shared-normal-regularization control (e.g., penalizing normal variation across neighbors), would be the natural next step.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations of the post-fix study should be stated plainly.

\textbf{Training set evaluation only.} All CD/NC numbers are computed on the 299-shape training set rather than a held-out test split. The post-fix retrains used \texttt{train\_split}~=~1.0 because the study is scoped around the interaction between Eikonal regularization, Fourier PE, and supervision ratio on the training objective; generalization to unseen shapes is a separate question that we do not address.

\textbf{Reduced epochs for the priority runs.} EXP-03 through EXP-06 and EXP-11 were trained for 1{,}500 epochs instead of the 3{,}000 used for EXP-01 and EXP-02, because the full post-fix pipeline had to fit inside an extended QoS window on the NTU TC2 cluster. All post-fix evaluations share the same $96^3$ marching-cubes resolution. Direct comparisons across post-fix rows (e.g.\ EXP-04 vs.\ EXP-03 vs.\ EXP-05) share the same reduced epoch setting; the cross-setting comparison (Eikonal runs vs.\ EXP-01) advantages EXP-01 on epochs. Repeating every run at the full 3{,}000-epoch setting would strengthen the claim but is not expected to reverse the direction of the results.

\textbf{Incomplete PE sweep.} EXP-07 (5\% + $L=6$), EXP-08 (10\% + $L=6$ + $\mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}$), EXP-09 (100\% + $L=6$), EXP-10 (100\% + $L=4$), and EXP-12 (5\% + $L=4$) were defined in the original plan but not retrained in the post-fix window. The PE conclusions therefore rest on the 10\% supervision point for both $L=4$ and $L=6$, with no data points at 100\% or 5\% in the post-fix pipeline.

\textbf{Single dataset and small dataset scale.} We train on 299 shapes across three ShapeNet categories. Both the label-efficiency and PE-trade-off claims may behave differently at larger scale, with more categories, or on non-ShapeNet geometry.

\textbf{Metric coverage.} The original plan included volumetric IoU and an Eikonal-deviation metric ($|\|\nabla f\| - 1|$); neither was computed in the post-fix pipeline. Our conclusions are based on CD and NC only.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We set out to test whether Eikonal regularization can substitute for signed-distance supervision in DeepSDF, and whether Fourier positional encoding helps or hurts in that regime. Early in the study we discovered that the preprocessing pipeline was producing SDF labels only in a thin $\pm 0.1$ band around each surface, which drove every configuration into a degenerate near-zero global solution and collapsed every reconstruction to a sphere. After extending preprocessing with a 20\% far-field slice sampled uniformly in the unit sphere (with exact signed distance via \texttt{trimesh.proximity.signed\_distance}) and retraining all 10 reported experiments from scratch, the experimental signal becomes interpretable.

The post-fix evidence supports three concrete findings. First, Eikonal regularization enables substantial label reduction: at 10\% supervision (EXP-04, 3-seed CD = 0.0315, CV = 0.047), reconstruction quality exceeds the 100\% non-Eikonal baseline (EXP-01, CD = 0.0361), with Normal Consistency preserved in the 0.72--0.75 range, and the 50\% and 5\% Eikonal points land in the same band. Second, Fourier PE is a CD/NC trade rather than a free quality gain: EXP-06 ($L=6$, 10\%) matches no-PE CD at the same ratio but halves NC (0.729~$\to$~0.517). Third, the common fallback of reducing PE frequency from $L=6$ to $L=4$ is strictly worse on CD at 10\% supervision (0.0308~$\to$~0.0432) without recovering NC to the no-PE level. All three claims are stable under 3-seed validation on the two workhorse configurations.

For future work, three directions are naturally indicated by these results. (i) Close the PE sweep at 5\% and 100\% supervision and at intermediate frequencies to characterize whether the NC cost depends on supervision ratio or on $L$. (ii) Test surface-normal-aware regularizers (e.g., penalizing normal variation across neighbors on the unsupervised pool) as a targeted counter to the PE NC loss. (iii) Replace Fourier PE with multi-resolution hash encodings~\cite{instantngp} or learned feature grids; the post-fix pipeline provides a clean baseline against which those alternatives can be evaluated without the preprocessing confound that had obscured earlier runs.
