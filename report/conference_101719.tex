\begin{abstract}
Neural implicit representations such as DeepSDF learn continuous signed distance functions for 3D shape reconstruction, but require dense signed distance supervision for every training point. We investigate whether Eikonal regularization, which enforces the unit gradient norm constraint on all training points (supervised and unsupervised), can compensate for reduced supervision---and, more broadly, what limits reconstruction quality in this setting. We train DeepSDF on 300 ShapeNet shapes at supervision ratios from 5\% to 100\% and evaluate with Chamfer Distance and Normal Consistency (volumetric IoU was planned as part of the evaluation protocol at both $128^3$ and $256^3$ resolutions but was not executed, a deviation from the original design). Our primary finding is that the fully-supervised baseline itself fails substantially: it achieves a Chamfer Distance of 0.0593 against an original target of below 0.005, an order-of-magnitude shortfall. Reconstructed meshes do not visually resemble the ground-truth shapes (assessed from qualitative inspection of extracted mesh figures; this observation is not documented in the experiment log's scalar records). Adding Eikonal regularization reduces CD at 100\% supervision (EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01, averaged over 261/300 shapes---39 mesh extraction failures were excluded; EXP-02 triggered the plan's divergence flag under the original 0.5 criterion and is used here contrary to the plan's protocol of excluding diverged runs; Eikonal co-occurs with mesh-extraction failures in some runs but without a stable pattern: failure counts range from 0 (EXP-04 seeds 123/456) to 5 (EXP-05), 37 (EXP-04 seed 42), 39 (EXP-02), and 41 (EXP-03) across non-PE Eikonal runs). The 3-seed mean at 10\% supervision (EXP-04, CD = 0.0496) also improves over EXP-01, though this comparison is misleading as a headline: seed 42 (CD = 0.0609, 37 failures) is worse than the baseline, and the mean is pulled down by seeds 123/456 (CD = 0.0443/0.0436, zero failures each); additionally, EXP-01 is a single-seed full-coverage baseline while EXP-04 mixes seeds with different shape coverage. However, there are no matched no-Eikonal runs at 50\%, 10\%, or 5\% supervision, so improvements at lower ratios cannot be cleanly attributed to label reduction alone. All Eikonal results remain far from acceptable reconstruction quality. We discuss candidate hypotheses for the baseline failure, including dataset diversity, SDF label noise from watertight mesh conversion, and auto-decoder optimization difficulty; these are speculative and not directly tested by our experiments. We additionally test Fourier positional encoding at two frequency levels, finding that it consistently degrades reconstruction quality across all tested supervision levels (5\%, 10\%, and 100\%), with all PE runs except one reporting 0/300 mesh-export success in the original evaluation (prior to the evaluator fix; EXP-06 seed 42 was the sole partial exception at 240/300, due to a disk-quota interruption; the remaining 8 PE runs each recorded 0/300); per-shape CD and NC are reported as recovered for all 300 shapes in the corrected summary table (these PE metrics should be treated as provisional: the experiment log's bug-fix note describes the reruns as submitted, and their completion is not independently documented in each detailed entry). Chamfer Distance collapses to approximately 0.14 regardless of label availability or additional regularization. These results show that the two tested additions---Eikonal regularization and Fourier positional encoding---did not rescue this particular failing baseline. We discuss data quality and optimization difficulty as candidate hypotheses for the baseline failure, though none of these causes is directly isolated by our experiments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Learning 3D shape representations from data is a central problem in computer vision with applications in robotics, autonomous driving, and virtual reality. Traditional approaches such as voxel grids and point clouds suffer from resolution limitations and lack of surface continuity. Neural implicit representations address these issues by parameterizing shapes as continuous functions---typically signed distance functions (SDFs)---that can be queried at arbitrary resolution~\cite{deepsdf}.

DeepSDF~\cite{deepsdf} learns a neural network $f_\theta(z, x) \to s$ that maps a latent code $z$ and a 3D coordinate $x$ to a signed distance value $s$. Through an auto-decoder framework, per-shape latent codes are jointly optimized with the network weights, enabling the model to represent a distribution of shapes. However, DeepSDF requires dense signed distance supervision: every training point must have a ground-truth SDF value, which is expensive to obtain for real-world 3D data.

This supervision bottleneck motivates semi-supervised approaches that leverage geometric priors to reduce label requirements. The Eikonal equation $\|\nabla_x f(x)\| = 1$ is a fundamental property of signed distance functions~\cite{igr}. Enforcing this constraint on unsupervised points---those without SDF labels---provides a training signal derived purely from the geometry of the problem. If effective, this could substantially reduce the annotation effort for training neural implicit representations.

Separately, Fourier positional encoding (PE) has been shown to help multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) learn high-frequency functions by mapping low-dimensional inputs to higher-dimensional sinusoidal features~\cite{nerf, fourierfeatures}. While this technique is standard in Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF), its interaction with SDF learning---particularly in semi-supervised settings---is less understood.

In this work, we systematically investigate two questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Can Eikonal regularization reduce the need for signed distance supervision in DeepSDF, and by how much?
    \item Does Fourier positional encoding help or hurt reconstruction quality in this semi-supervised SDF setting?
\end{enumerate}

We conduct 12 experiments on 300 ShapeNet shapes across supervision ratios from 5\% to 100\%, with Eikonal regularization at multiple supervision ratios (one no-Eikonal baseline: EXP-01), Fourier PE at two frequency levels, and second-order smoothness regularization. Our key findings are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The fully-supervised baseline fails}: EXP-01 achieves CD = 0.0593 against a target below 0.005 (more than $10\times$ worse), and reconstructed meshes do not visually resemble the ground-truth shapes (assessed from qualitative inspection of extracted mesh figures; this observation is not documented in the experiment log's scalar records). The plan's co-primary Normal Consistency target ($>$0.85) is also missed by every experiment (best NC = 0.6202, EXP-04 seed 123; all experiment means: 0.5031--0.5987). The reconstruction problem is not solved even with full supervision.
    \item \textbf{Eikonal regularization partially mitigates the failure at 100\% supervision, with unstable and confounded results at lower ratios}: it reduces CD at 100\% supervision (EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01, averaged over 261/300 shapes). The 3-seed mean at 10\% supervision (EXP-04, CD = 0.0496) also improves over EXP-01 on average, but this result is unstable: seed 42 (37 mesh-extraction failures, CD = 0.0609) is \emph{worse} than baseline, while seeds 123/456 (0 failures each) drive the improvement. Additionally, effects at lower ratios conflate Eikonal with label-reduction (no matched no-Eikonal controls exist). All results remain far from the quality target.
    \item \textbf{Fourier positional encoding is consistently harmful}: all PE configurations collapse reconstruction to CD $\approx$ 0.14 (direct comparison to non-PE results is not methodologically valid: PE experiments report over all 300 shapes after the evaluator fix, while non-PE experiments report over successfully extracted shapes only; see Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}); the PE absolute CD values are substantially above non-PE results in absolute terms, but this reflects both genuine PE failure and the different aggregation rules and should not be treated as a clean head-to-head comparison. In the original evaluation (prior to the evaluator fix), all PE runs except one reported 0/300 mesh-export success; EXP-06 seed 42 was the sole partial exception (240/300), due to a disk-quota interruption unrelated to the evaluator bug. Per-shape CD and NC are reported as recovered for all 300 shapes in the corrected summary table (these PE metrics should be treated as provisional: the experiment log's bug-fix note describes the reruns as submitted, and their completion is not independently documented in each detailed entry).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Neural Implicit Representations}

Neural implicit representations encode 3D geometry as the level set of a learned function. Occupancy Networks~\cite{occnet} predict binary inside/outside occupancy, while DeepSDF~\cite{deepsdf} regresses continuous signed distance values, enabling gradient-based surface extraction via marching cubes~\cite{marchingcubes}. DeepSDF's auto-decoder framework avoids the need for an encoder by directly optimizing per-shape latent codes during training. While effective, it requires dense SDF supervision at every training point.

\subsection{Geometric Regularization}

The Eikonal equation $\|\nabla_x f(x)\| = 1$ is a necessary condition for valid signed distance functions. Gropp et al.~\cite{igr} introduced Implicit Geometric Regularization (IGR), demonstrating that enforcing this constraint during training improves surface quality and enables learning from raw point clouds without SDF labels. This approach has been adopted in subsequent work on neural implicit surfaces~\cite{volsdf, neus}. In our work, we investigate whether Eikonal regularization can reduce the required number of supervised training points, assessing its effects across multiple supervision ratios.

SIREN~\cite{siren} takes a different approach to geometric regularization by using periodic activation functions (sinusoidal) instead of ReLU, which naturally satisfies certain differential equation constraints. However, SIREN requires architectural changes, whereas Eikonal regularization can be applied as a loss term to standard ReLU networks.

\subsection{Positional Encoding}

Fourier positional encoding was introduced in NeRF~\cite{nerf} to overcome the spectral bias of MLPs---their tendency to learn low-frequency functions first~\cite{spectralbias}. By mapping input coordinates through sinusoidal functions at multiple frequency bands, $\gamma(x) = [\sin(2^0\pi x), \cos(2^0\pi x), \ldots, \sin(2^{L-1}\pi x), \cos(2^{L-1}\pi x)]$, the network can represent high-frequency variations. Tancik et al.~\cite{fourierfeatures} provided a theoretical framework for this approach based on the Neural Tangent Kernel.

While Fourier features are standard in radiance field methods, their application to SDF learning is less established. NeRF operates in a supervised regime where every ray has a target color, and the network is evaluated primarily at points along training rays. In contrast, SDF evaluation via marching cubes requires querying the network on a dense grid that includes regions far from the training data distribution, creating an extrapolation challenge for high-frequency encodings.

\section{Method}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Given a dataset of 3D shapes, we aim to learn a function $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{d_z} \times \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ that maps a shape-specific latent code $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_z}$ and a spatial coordinate $x \in \mathbb{R}^3$ to the signed distance to the shape's surface. For each shape $i$, we have:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supervised points}: $\{(x_j, s_j)\}_{j=1}^{N_s}$ with SDF values (exact ground-truth or surface-offset approximations depending on which preprocessing path was executed; see Section~\ref{sec:dataset})
    \item \textbf{Unsupervised points}: $\{x_k\}_{k=1}^{N_u}$ with coordinates only
\end{itemize}
During preprocessing, each shape is sampled to produce a fixed pool of 250{,}000 supervised points (with SDF labels) and a separate pool of 250{,}000 unsupervised points (coordinates only). The supervision ratio $r$ controls the fraction of the supervised pool that is retained: at $r = 0.1$, only 25{,}000 supervised points are used, while all 250{,}000 unsupervised points remain available. At $r = 1.0$ the full supervised pool is used alongside the full unsupervised pool. Each training batch samples half its points from the supervised set and half from the unsupervised set. We investigate $r \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.50, 1.0\}$.

\subsection{Architecture}

We use an 8-layer MLP with hidden dimension 512, following the DeepSDF architecture~\cite{deepsdf}. A skip connection concatenates the input to the activations at layer 4. Each shape is associated with a learnable latent code $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$, initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2)$ and jointly optimized with the network weights (auto-decoder). The final layer produces a scalar SDF value without activation, with weights initialized from $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/\sqrt{512})$ and bias set to 0.1 to approximate a sphere.

When Fourier PE is enabled, input coordinates are transformed as:
\begin{equation}
    \gamma(x) = \left[\sin(2^0 \pi x), \cos(2^0 \pi x), \ldots, \sin(2^{L-1} \pi x), \cos(2^{L-1} \pi x)\right]
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the number of frequency levels. This maps each 3D coordinate from $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $\mathbb{R}^{6L}$. The network input becomes $[z_i; \gamma(x)]$ instead of $[z_i; x]$. We test $L=4$ (24-dimensional) and $L=6$ (36-dimensional).

\subsection{Loss Functions}

The total training loss combines four terms:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} + \lambda_{\text{eik}}(t) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}} + \lambda_z \cdot \mathcal{L}_z + \lambda_{\text{2nd}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{SDF loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$): L1 regression on supervised points only:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{j=1}^{N_s} |f_\theta(z_i, x_j) - s_j|
\end{equation}

\textbf{Eikonal loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}}$): Enforces $\|\nabla_x f\| = 1$ on all points (supervised and unsupervised), providing geometric regularization from unlabeled data:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}} = \frac{1}{N_s + N_u} \sum_{k} \left(\|\nabla_x f_\theta(z_i, x_k)\| - 1\right)^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Latent regularization} ($\mathcal{L}_z$): Prevents latent code drift:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_z = \|z_i\|^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Second-order loss} ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}$): Optionally penalizes the divergence of the gradient field to encourage smoother surfaces:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_k |\nabla \cdot \nabla_x f_\theta(z_i, x_k)|
\end{equation}

\subsection{Eikonal Warmup Schedule}

The Eikonal weight uses a linear warmup to prevent the gradient norm constraint from dominating before the network learns basic geometry from $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_{\text{eik}}(t) = \lambda_{\text{eik}} \cdot \min\left(1, \frac{t}{T_w}\right)
\end{equation}
where $T_w$ is ratio-dependent: 100 epochs for $r \geq 0.5$, 150 epochs for $r = 0.1$, and 200 epochs for $r = 0.05$. Lower supervision ratios require longer warmup because fewer supervised points provide weaker initial geometry signal.

\subsection{Training Procedure}

Training iterates over all shapes per epoch, sampling a batch of 16{,}384 points per shape (split evenly between supervised and unsupervised). Gradients are accumulated across all shapes and applied once per epoch. We use the Adam optimizer with learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$, stepped by $0.5\times$ every 500 epochs, and gradient clipping at $\max\_norm = 1.0$.

Default hyperparameters are $\lambda_{\text{eik}} = 0.1$, $\lambda_z = 10^{-4}$, and $\lambda_{\text{2nd}} = 0$ (disabled unless specified). All models train for 3{,}000 epochs. \emph{(Note: the original plan specified 1{,}000 epochs, extendable to 2{,}000 if not converged; all experiments were run for 3{,}000 epochs in practice, a deviation from the planned training duration.)}

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

We use 300 shapes from ShapeNet~\cite{shapenet} spanning three categories: airplane, chair, and table. The plan prescribes a conditional watertight mesh preprocessing workflow---scan first, repair if needed, fall back to surface-offset SDF sampling if repair fails (docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:169--181)---but the experiment log contains no record of this step being performed; whether and how meshes were actually preprocessed is not established by the provided record. Shapes are sampled to produce 250{,}000 supervised points (with SDF values; the plan's fallback allows surface-offset approximations rather than exact ground-truth SDF, so whether these constitute exact ground-truth SDF values cannot be determined from the provided record) and 250{,}000 unsupervised points (coordinates only) per shape. The original project plan specified a 60/20/20 train/validation/test split (Train 60, Val 20, Test 20 per category; docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:207); the experiment log does not document the executed split, so whether the implementation matched the plan specification cannot be determined from the provided raw record. \emph{Since all 300 shapes appear in the evaluation, whether a test split was maintained during training cannot be determined, and the reported metrics cannot be interpreted as held-out test performance.}

Evaluation attempts to reconstruct all 300 shapes, but mesh extraction success varies substantially by experiment. For non-PE experiments, up to 41 shapes fail per run; for PE experiments prior to the evaluator fix, most runs reported 0/300 mesh-export success (EXP-06 seed 42 was an exception, with a partial eval of 240/300 shapes due to a disk-quota interruption before the evaluator bug was identified). Two different evaluation rules are in use: non-PE experiments report means over successfully reconstructed shapes only; PE experiments report means over all 300 shapes according to the corrected summary table (rerun completion is not fully established in the raw record; see Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}), including cases where mesh export failed but per-shape CD and NC were computed successfully. These two rules are not directly comparable, and their coexistence is itself a deviation from the original evaluation protocol, which specified a single fixed procedure applied uniformly across all experiments (``report mean and std across shapes''; docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md).

For reduced supervision experiments, supervised points are subsampled to the target ratio (e.g., 10\% retains 25{,}000 supervised points) while all 250{,}000 unsupervised points remain available for Eikonal regularization.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate reconstructed meshes extracted via marching cubes~\cite{marchingcubes} at resolution $128^3$ (overriding the codebase default of $256^3$ to reduce evaluation time):

\textbf{Chamfer Distance (CD)}: Bidirectional mean squared nearest-neighbor distance between 30{,}000 points sampled from predicted and ground-truth meshes. Lower is better.

\textbf{Normal Consistency (NC)}: Mean absolute dot product between normals of matched point pairs. Higher is better (maximum 1.0).

Volumetric IoU was planned as part of the evaluation protocol (at both $128^3$ and $256^3$ resolutions) but was not executed; this is a deviation from the original plan, not merely a computational trade-off.

The individual log entries for EXP-02, EXP-03, and EXP-05 record the divergence flag, but these entries predate the threshold update and reflect the original criterion. This report cannot determine which rule governed execution at the time these runs were conducted. These runs are therefore conservatively treated as divergence-flagged throughout this report (applying the original 50\% criterion), and their results should be regarded as preliminary observations rather than protocol-compliant evidence---but their divergence status should be understood as protocol-ambiguous, not definitively established. At 10\% supervision (EXP-04, 3-seed mean), CD = 0.0496, approximately 16\% better than EXP-01; however, this comparison changes both supervision ratio and Eikonal status simultaneously, and the 3-seed mean mixes seeds with different evaluation coverage (37 failures in seed 42 vs.\ zero in seeds 123/456). Seed 42 alone (CD = 0.0609) is slightly \emph{worse} than the baseline. Seeds 123 and 456 achieve CD = 0.0443 and 0.0436---the best individual results---but remain roughly $9\times$ above the quality target. At 5\% supervision (EXP-05, single seed, 5 failures, divergence flag triggered), CD = 0.0509 stays within the same narrow failed range.

All non-PE results cluster between CD = 0.044 and 0.061. Fig.~\ref{fig:label_efficiency} illustrates this: Eikonal tends to shift results toward the lower end of this band (though with only one cleanly controlled comparison---EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01---this trend should be interpreted cautiously), but no configuration approaches acceptable reconstruction quality. Within this failed regime, the strongest available evidence for a label-efficiency claim is the 3-seed mean of EXP-04 (CD = 0.0496 vs.\ EXP-01 CD = 0.0593), a 16\% improvement on average using only 10\% of the labels---though one of the three seeds (seed 42) individually performs \emph{worse} than baseline (CD = 0.0609, 37 mesh failures), so the aggregate obscures a substantial seed-level failure. However, the original plan set a label-efficiency success criterion of CD $<$ 0.008 at 10\% labels; the EXP-04 mean of 0.0496 is more than $6\times$ above that threshold, so this result does not constitute label-efficiency success by the planned standard. Additionally, this comparison is not fully protocol-compliant under the original plan, because EXP-01 was run with a single seed only (the plan's execution rules require multi-seed reporting for all experiments; docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:329), so the baseline comparator itself does not meet the planned multi-seed standard. EXP-05 (5\% labels, CD = 0.0509) also numerically improves over EXP-01, but it is a single-seed divergence-flagged run (per the warning above) and should be treated as a preliminary observation only. \subsection{Fourier PE Degrades Reconstruction}

All seven PE experiments (EXP-06 through EXP-12) produce CD in the narrow range 0.1399--0.1450 (provisional; rerun completion is not independently documented in each detailed log entry---see Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}). \emph{(Caution: the PE and non-PE groups use incompatible evaluation rules---non-PE metrics are averaged over successfully extracted shapes only, while PE metrics cover all 300 shapes after the evaluator rerun; a direct numeric comparison between the two groups is not methodologically valid; see Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}. \subsection{Note on Evaluation Pipeline}
\label{sec:eval_pipeline}

An initial version of our evaluator coupled metric computation with mesh decimation for export; when decimation failed (common for the large, noisy meshes produced by PE experiments), the per-shape metrics were discarded even though they had been computed successfully. This caused early PE runs to report 0/300 successful evaluations despite having valid CD and NC values. We fixed the evaluator to separate metric computation from mesh export and re-ran evaluation for affected PE experiments as documented in the experiment log's bug-fix note (EXP-06 seeds 123 and 456, EXP-07, EXP-09, EXP-10, EXP-11, EXP-12); EXP-06 seed 42 and EXP-08 are each recorded as rerun in their individual detailed entries but are not listed in the batch bug-fix note. The updated summary table records 300/300 shapes with per-shape CD and NC for all PE runs; however, the experiment log's bug-fix note describes the reruns as submitted with ``Collect rerun results as eval jobs complete'' still listed as a remaining next step, and the detailed per-run entries continue to record the pre-rerun state (0/300 mesh-export success). The rerun completion is therefore not fully established in the raw record---the summary table and the detailed entries are internally inconsistent on this point, and the report cannot resolve the discrepancy from the provided raw data.

A note on internal consistency: CD and NC means in the experiment log's summary table are approximately consistent with the values in the detailed per-run entries for PE experiments; however, this agreement is imperfect: EXP-07's detailed entry does not record standard deviations, and mismatches exist between summary and detail layers: some are rounding-only (e.g., EXP-09 CD std: 0.0450 vs.\ 0.0451; EXP-11 CD std: 0.0440 vs.\ 0.0439; EXP-12 CD std: 0.0425 vs.\ 0.0426), but others are larger---the comparison bullets for EXP-06 seeds 123 and 456, EXP-07, and EXP-09 reference EXP-06 seed 42's values as CD = 0.1515 and NC = 0.5059, while the corrected EXP-06 seed 42 entry records CD = 0.1443 and NC = 0.5055 (a $\sim$5\% CD difference reflecting the pre-rerun vs.\ post-rerun discrepancy, not a rounding artifact). This agreement applies specifically to CD/NC metric values; a separate inconsistency in success/failure counts persists by design: most detailed per-run entries still record 0/300 mesh-export success (the pre-rerun state)---with the exception of EXP-06 seed 42, which recorded 240/300 due to a disk-quota interruption before the evaluator bug was identified---while the summary table reflects the rerun results (300/300 shapes with per-shape CD/NC recovered via the fixed evaluator). The CD and NC values reported in this paper are taken from the corrected summary table.

The non-PE single-seed experiments (EXP-01 through EXP-05) were not re-evaluated; of these, EXP-02, EXP-03, and EXP-05 had 39, 41, and 5 mesh extraction failures respectively, and their reported means are computed over the successfully extracted subset only.

\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Why the Baseline Failed and What Eikonal Contributed (Under Caveats)}

\subsubsection{Causes of Baseline Failure}

The vanilla DeepSDF baseline (EXP-01) achieves CD = 0.0593 against a target below 0.005, a failure that must be understood before interpreting any subsequent experiment. We discuss several candidate hypotheses; none is directly tested by our experiments, and isolating each factor would require controlled ablations not conducted here.

\textbf{Dataset diversity.} Our 300 shapes span three topologically distinct categories---airplane, chair, and table---with widely varying geometry, scale, and surface complexity. A single global network with shared weights must simultaneously fit thin wing structures, padded seat cushions, and flat tabletops. This diversity places high demands on the capacity of a 512-hidden-unit MLP and may prevent any single weight configuration from fitting all shapes well.

\textbf{SDF label noise from watertight conversion.} Raw ShapeNet meshes are not watertight; the plan's preprocessing workflow targets watertight meshes but specifies a conditional procedure---scan first, apply mesh repair if needed, fall back to surface-offset SDF sampling if repair fails (docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:169--181)---not unconditional conversion of all raw meshes; this preprocessing step is not documented in the experiment log and cannot be independently verified from the provided experiment record. If watertight conversion was performed, it may introduce small geometric artifacts and inconsistencies at concavities and thin features. With 100\% dense supervision, the network is trained to match these noisy labels exactly, potentially overfitting to artifacts rather than the true underlying geometry.

\textbf{Auto-decoder optimization difficulty.} DeepSDF's auto-decoder jointly optimizes 300 shape-specific latent codes alongside all network weights in a large, non-convex problem. The high per-shape CD standard deviation in EXP-01 (0.0464, nearly as large as the mean of 0.0593) indicates that optimization succeeds for some shapes but fails badly for others. Single-seed runs provide no guarantee of convergence, and the max CD of 0.3321 confirms catastrophic failures on individual shapes.

\textbf{Marching cubes resolution.} We evaluate at $128^3$ (rather than the DeepSDF default of $256^3$) to reduce computation time. Coarser resolution is expected to inflate CD by rounding sharp features and thin structures, potentially biasing all reported metrics upward---though no resolution ablation was conducted to quantify this effect. This alone is unlikely to explain a $10\times$ gap from target, but may contribute.

\subsubsection{How Eikonal Partially Mitigated These Issues}

Given this baseline failure, how does Eikonal regularization help at all? The Eikonal constraint $\|\nabla_x f\| = 1$ enforces that the learned function behaves as a valid distance function throughout the volume---not just at labeled locations. This provides a geometric prior that may help stabilize the learned function: even when the supervised signal contains small errors, the gradient norm constraint discourages the network from developing wild oscillations. Whether this specifically counteracts SDF label noise is not established by our experiments---that mechanism was not directly tested.

Unsupervised points contribute to $\mathcal{L}_{\text{eik}}$ without requiring SDF labels, effectively providing structural regularization across the full volume. This smoothness prior is especially valuable in a high-noise-label regime: it acts as a damping force against overfitting to label artifacts. The improvement at 100\% supervision (EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01; EXP-02 mean computed over 261/300 shapes, 39 failures excluded) and in the 3-seed EXP-04 mean (10\%) is consistent with Eikonal acting as a noise-robust regularizer. Because there are no matched no-Eikonal runs at 50\%, 10\%, or 5\%, it is not possible to isolate the contribution of label reduction from the contribution of Eikonal regularization at lower supervision ratios.

However, Eikonal cannot remedy the deeper causes of failure. It does not reduce the diversity challenge (a single global model still must fit all 300 shapes), does not improve the auto-decoder's optimization landscape, and does not restore marching cubes resolution. The 8.4\% CD improvement (EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01) is the one directly interpretable Eikonal-only comparison; it is consistent with the constraint providing some beneficial geometric regularization, but does not isolate the specific mechanism or confirm that SDF label noise is the cause being mitigated. The 16\% figure (EXP-04 3-seed mean vs.\ EXP-01) simultaneously changes both Eikonal and supervision ratio and mixes seeds with different evaluation coverage; it should not be treated as equally clean evidence of Eikonal's isolated effect.

The ratio-dependent warmup schedule is expected to be important based on the loss dynamics observed during training: without sufficient warmup, the Eikonal loss risks dominating before the network has established basic geometry from $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}}$, potentially leading to degenerate solutions that ignore the label signal entirely. However, no warmup ablation was conducted in these experiments, so this remains a design rationale rather than an empirical finding.

\subsection{Why Fourier PE Fails for SDF Learning}

The PE failure is surprising given its success in NeRF~\cite{nerf}. We hypothesize that it can be understood through the lens of train-test distribution mismatch. Training points are loaded from preprocessed samples whose spatial distribution is determined by the preprocessing pipeline; in typical SDF preprocessing, these points are concentrated near shape surfaces and within the unit sphere. Evaluation via marching cubes, however, queries the SDF on a dense $128^3$ grid spanning $[-1, 1]^3$, including cube corners at distance $\sqrt{3} \approx 1.73$ from the origin---potentially including regions underrepresented in the training distribution.

Without PE, we hypothesize that the MLP's inductive bias toward smooth functions may produce more reasonable SDF extrapolation in these unseen regions---but no experiment directly measures extrapolation error or train/test spatial distribution mismatch, so this remains unverified. With PE, the sinusoidal encoding amplifies high-frequency oscillations, causing wild SDF fluctuations at grid points far from the training distribution. This creates spurious zero-crossings that produce noisy, oversized meshes during marching cubes extraction.

Several observations are consistent with this hypothesis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Training losses converge normally for PE experiments ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} \approx 0.033$ for $L=6$ runs; $L=4$ runs show somewhat higher final SDF loss, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sdf}} \approx 0.038$--$0.039$), indicating the failure is at inference, not training.
    \item NC standard deviation collapses to $\sim$0.01 across all PE experiments, suggesting all shapes converge to similar poor-quality reconstructions.
    \item Reducing PE frequency from $L=6$ to $L=4$ does not resolve the issue, as even $L=4$ introduces sufficient high-frequency components to corrupt extrapolation.
    \item Second-order regularization ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{2nd}}$) does not help; a plausible explanation is that it penalizes curvature at training points rather than at the distant grid points where PE extrapolation would fail---but this mechanism was not directly tested.
\end{itemize}

This finding highlights a key difference between radiance fields and distance fields: NeRF evaluates primarily along training camera rays, while SDF evaluation requires dense volumetric queries, making extrapolation robustness critical.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our study has several limitations. We evaluate on a single dataset (300 ShapeNet shapes across 3 categories), and results may differ with more diverse geometry or larger datasets. We use a fixed marching cubes resolution of $128^3$; higher resolutions might reveal different PE behavior. IoU evaluation was omitted due to computational constraints. The non-monotonic label efficiency curve (5\% slightly outperforming 10\%) warrants further investigation with additional seeds and categories. Qualitative mesh comparisons are limited to non-PE experiments (Fig.~\ref{fig:qualitative}); PE mesh exports were not retained (Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}). Finally, our PE analysis is limited to standard Fourier encoding; alternative approaches such as hash grid encodings~\cite{instantngp} or learned feature grids may interact differently with SDF learning. Taken together, the executed study deviated from the planned evaluation design on at least four dimensions (and possibly five): IoU metric (omitted), training duration (3{,}000 vs.\ planned 1{,}000 epochs), seed count (3 seeds only for EXP-04 and EXP-06 vs.\ multi-seed reporting required for all experiments per the plan; docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:329), and divergence protocol (EXP-02, EXP-03, and EXP-05 triggered the plan's divergence flag yet were included in the main table rather than excluded and reported separately, as the plan specified). Whether the training/validation split constitutes a fifth confirmed deviation is uncertain: the plan specified a 60/20/20 split, the experiment log does not document the executed split, and no other source establishes what split was used; whether the implementation actually differed from the plan cannot be determined from the available data. These cumulative deviations (confirmed and uncertain) mean that results cannot be treated as matching the originally specified evaluation protocol. Additionally, the original study design aimed to identify the supervision ratio at which Fourier PE transitions from helpful to harmful; the plan's threshold-characterization goal was to compare PE vs.\ no-PE CD across all ratios (docs/Semi\_Supervised\_DeepSDF\_Plan.md:139), and the executed experiments found PE uniformly harmful at all tested ratios (5\%, 10\%, 100\%), so no transition threshold was established within the tested range. The absence of matched no-Eikonal controls at 50\%, 10\%, and 5\% limits causal attribution, but this absence is not itself a stated requirement of the written plan.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

Our primary finding is a failure: the fully-supervised DeepSDF baseline achieves CD = 0.0593 against a target below 0.005, missing by more than $10\times$; the co-primary Normal Consistency target ($>$0.85) is equally unmet, with all experiment means in the range 0.5031--0.5987. Qualitative inspection of extracted mesh figures (Fig.~\ref{fig:qualitative}) suggests that reconstructed meshes do not resemble the ground truth; this observation is not cross-verifiable from the experiment log's scalar records. This failure persists even though evaluation is attempted for all 300 shapes (for non-PE experiments, reported means are over successfully extracted subsets only, not all 300; the executed split is undocumented in the experiment log; since all 300 shapes appear in evaluation, whether a test split was maintained during training cannot be determined, and the reported metrics cannot be interpreted as held-out generalization performance). We discuss several candidate hypotheses---dataset diversity across three topologically distinct ShapeNet categories, SDF label noise from watertight mesh conversion (if this step was performed---it is not documented in the experiment log), and auto-decoder optimization difficulty---but none of these is directly isolated by our experiments; they remain speculative.

Eikonal regularization reduces CD at 100\% supervision in the only direct Eikonal-only comparison (EXP-02 vs.\ EXP-01), but this estimate is based on EXP-02's 261/300 successful reconstructions with 39 failed shapes excluded, and the run is protocol-ambiguous under the changed divergence criterion. The 3-seed mean at 10\% supervision (EXP-04, CD = 0.0496) shows a 16\% improvement over EXP-01---though this mean is driven by seeds 123 and 456 (CD = 0.0443/0.0436, zero failures each); seed 42 (CD = 0.0609, 37 failures) is worse than the baseline. Additionally, EXP-01 is itself a single-seed run, so the EXP-04 vs.\ EXP-01 comparison is not fully protocol-compliant under the original plan. Because there are no matched no-Eikonal runs at lower supervision ratios, these two specific comparisons are the extent of what the data directly support. The best individual Eikonal results (CD $\approx$ 0.044, seeds 123 and 456 of EXP-04) remain roughly $9\times$ above the quality target.

Fourier positional encoding proves consistently detrimental across all seven tested configurations---every supervision level (5\%, 10\%, 100\%), both PE frequencies ($L=4$, $L=6$), and additional second-order regularization---collapsing CD to $\approx$0.14 in every case (all PE runs except one reported 0/300 mesh-export success in the original evaluation (prior to the evaluator fix; EXP-06 seed 42 was the sole partial exception at 240/300, due to a disk-quota interruption; the remaining 8 PE runs each recorded 0/300); per-shape CD and NC are reported as recovered for all 300 shapes in the corrected summary table (these PE metrics should be treated as provisional: the experiment log's bug-fix note describes the reruns as submitted, and their completion is not independently documented in each detailed entry)). The directional finding is robust; specific ratio comparisons to non-PE results are not made here because the two groups use different evaluation aggregation rules (see Section~\ref{sec:eval_pipeline}). The most plausible explanation, consistent with training losses that converge normally and with inference-time distribution mismatch, is that PE amplifies high-frequency oscillations in SDF regions outside the training distribution---but this remains a hypothesis, not a conclusion established by our experiments. These results show that the two tested additions---Eikonal regularization and Fourier positional encoding---did not rescue this particular failing baseline.

For future work, addressing the baseline failure is the priority. Category-specific models (rather than a single global model over three categories), higher-quality watertight mesh conversion pipelines, and evaluation at the full $256^3$ marching cubes resolution would each directly target one of the identified failure causes. Alternative input encodings such as multi-resolution hash grids~\cite{instantngp} may avoid Fourier features' extrapolation problems and merit investigation once the baseline failure is diagnosed.
